## Transcript-Derived Principles

### Editing Needs Reader Amnesia

Pressfield describes rereading as if encountering the story for the first time and asking whether it still pulls the reader in. For agents, this means judging the target beat by the reader's current knowledge, not the writer's private intent.

Agent rule:

- Before changing prose, identify what a first-time reader knows at that exact point.

### Draft Passes Use Different Tools

The Pressfield interview distinguishes larger structural realizations from later chisel work. Housel describes sentence-level attention while writing. The combined lesson is scale awareness.

Agent rule:

- Do not use an axe when the user asked for a chisel.
- Escalate scope only when the target cannot work locally.

### Story Components Have Jobs

The Pressfield material names components like inciting incident, midpoint, all-is-lost moment, epiphany, villain pressure, genre convention, and scene function. A surgical edit should preserve the job of surrounding components.

Agent rule:

- If revising a line inside a scene, do not accidentally remove the scene's structural job.

### Genre Conventions Are Load-Bearing

The transcripts frame conventions as recurring story machinery that can be spun in fresh ways, not discarded out of embarrassment.

Agent rule:

- Preserve genre machinery unless the user explicitly asks to subvert or replace it.

### Cut What Readers Skip

Housel's editing discussion emphasizes removing rambling and unnecessary material. In surgical mode, this supports precise cuts, not broad rewrites.

Agent rule:

- Cut direct waste inside the allowed radius.
- Leave unrelated prose untouched, even if it could be improved.
