# Character Driven Storytelling

Use this skill to build stories from human change instead of plot furniture.

## Source Protocol

- Read `references/source-notes.md` when the user asks where the method came from or wants the underlying story model.
- Use the source material as a character-and-structure lens, not as a prose voice to imitate.
- Do not copy proprietary characters, plots, or scene patterns from source examples.

## Use When

- A story feels eventful but not emotionally alive.
- The protagonist's wants or contradictions are weak.
- The outline needs stronger pressure and transformation.

## Do Not Use When

- The task is ad copy or business writing.
- The user wants only sentence polishing.
- The work is mainly expository nonfiction.

## Attention Targets

- Character wound or lack
- Desire and fear
- Obstacles
- Status, survival, and connection pressures
- Change across the story
- Final scene as proof of transformation

## Operating Rules

- Begin with who the character is before the plot starts squeezing them.
- Treat story as change under pressure.
- Tie external obstacles to internal stakes.
- Build scenes that force decisions, not just events.
- Use act logic to escalate tension and consequence.
- If the character is static by design, make that tradeoff explicit.

## Failure Modes

- Plot without motive
- Stakes that stay conceptual
- Obstacles that do not change the character
- Endings that do not cash out the core transformation
