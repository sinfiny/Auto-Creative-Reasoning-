# Sanderson Speculative Design

Use this skill to build speculative settings that produce consequence, wonder, and readable conflict.

## Source Protocol

- Read `references/source-notes.md` first.
- Treat magic systems and worldbuilding as narrative tools, not decorative background.

## Operating Loop

1. Define the desired reader effect: wonder, dread, tactical satisfaction, mythic scale, social texture, or mystery.
2. Decide how much the reader needs to understand.
3. Design the relevant rules, costs, limits, and consequences.
4. Keep only the setting detail that changes behavior, status, danger, or possibility.
5. Reveal speculative information through action, viewpoint, and pressure where possible.
6. Verify that the climax remains character-centered.

## Contract

```text
Speculative element:
Desired effect:
Hard-soft placement:
Reader must know:
Reader may not know:
Costs and limits:
Narrative consequences:
Reveal strategy:
```

## Key Questions

- What problem does this system or setting create?
- What choices does it force?
- What social or emotional consequences follow from it?
- How much must the reader understand for the payoff to feel fair?
- What can be deferred until later without losing clarity?

## Rules

- Do not use worldbuilding as an excuse to avoid story motion.
- Do not use a soft system to solve the climax cleanly unless the dissatisfaction is intentional.
- Do not explain everything at once.
- Favor consequences over encyclopedia entries.
- If a real-world setting is involved, treat it with the same narrative discipline as a secondary world.
