# Source Notes

Primary lectures:

- Lecture 13: how publishing works
- Lecture 14: what publishing does for you
- Lecture 15: demystifying the publishing process
- Lecture 16: final Q&A on breaking in

Condensed notes:

- Modern publishing is shaped by consolidation, best-seller economics, ebooks, and platform control.
- Indie publishing had a major gold-rush phase, but current success usually requires more research, assets, and marketing skill.
- Agents can add value through negotiation, rights handling, market knowledge, and access.
- Conferences are useful when targeted and researched, not as random hope tourism.
- Do not pay agents.
- Rights, special editions, hybrid careers, and foreign sales matter and should be handled intentionally.
- Mailing lists are a durable audience asset because the author controls them directly.
- Platform writing only makes sense when the platform itself adds value and fits the author.
- Beta readers are a test audience; alpha readers can be more directive.
- Querying generally makes sense after several serious drafts, not infinite polishing.

Operational implications:

- When the user asks for a path, ask what they want from the career.
- When they ask about audience building, default to mailing list and sustainable communication.
- When they ask about agents, help them research individuals, not abstractions.
