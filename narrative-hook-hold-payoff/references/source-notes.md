## Transcript-Derived Principles

### Inciting Incident Points Toward Climax

Pressfield describes an inciting incident as the moment when the story starts moving and the audience can glimpse the kind of climax being promised.

Agent rule:

- A hook should not merely be interesting; it should imply a future collision.

### Midpoint Raises Meaning

The midpoint is treated as a turn where stakes rise and the story changes form or meaning. It often forces the hero to take sides.

Agent rule:

- The Hold should not repeat pressure at the same level. It should transform what the pressure means.

### All-Is-Lost Creates Enclosed Pressure

The all-is-lost beat puts the hero at the mercy of the villain, system, flaw, environment, or consequence. The value is not torment for its own sake; it forces revelation and problem solving.

Agent rule:

- Pressure must produce choice, learning, cost, or revelation.

### Epiphany Follows Pressure

The epiphany is earned by the all-is-lost pressure. It gives the hero or reader a new understanding that makes the next move possible.

Agent rule:

- Do not hand the character insight before the scene has paid for it.

### Genre Convention Plus Spin

The transcript argues for leaning into durable story principles while finding a fresh spin. Familiar machinery is not the enemy; stale execution is.

Agent rule:

- Preserve the reader's genre bargain and refresh it through character, setting, cost, or consequence.

### First Drafts Need Forward Motion

Pressfield's first-draft model prioritizes covering the canvas and moving forward. For AI generation, this is useful only after success criteria exist.

Agent rule:

- Use Hook/Hold/Payoff as the target, then draft forward without stopping to decorate every corner.

### Sixty Scenes As Clothesline

Pressfield describes using scene cards as a flexible clothesline: known beginning, major turns, ending, and blanks to fill.

Agent rule:

- For chapters or arcs, plan fixed anchor beats first, then fill intervening beats only as needed.
