# Source Notes

Primary source: David Perell's interview with Morgan Housel, "How to Write Insanely Well" (`o84GXnrHdgg`).

Caption pass: English captions were fetched with `yt-dlp`, cleaned in `/tmp`, and reviewed for the sections on point, brevity, storytelling, reader attention, editing, sentences, and reader empathy. The cleaned transcript was about 27.5k words.

## Transcript-Derived Principles

### Make The Point Visible

The interview repeatedly returns to a reader-facing question: what is the point, and how fast can the writer make it legible? For fiction agents, translate that into: before drafting a scene, name the point of the beat. If the point cannot be stated, the scene is not ready to generate.

Agent rule:

- Do not start prose until the beat's job is stated in one sentence.
- If the scene has many possible points, surface the tradeoff and pick one.

### Story First, Then Generalization

Housel describes finding the story first and then asking what broader lesson or point it carries. For fiction, the concrete situation should drive the abstraction, not the reverse.

Agent rule:

- Anchor reasoning in events, choices, and constraints already present.
- Do not invent a theme and then force characters to act it out.

### Reader Attention Is Fragile

The transcript emphasizes impatient readers, quick drop-off, and the danger of rambling. For an AI writing loop, this means the agent should not bury motive, conflict, or contradiction under decorative setup.

Agent rule:

- Put conflict, desire, or pressure near the front of a scene plan.
- Treat unclear motive as a reader-attention failure.

### Brevity Means No Rambling

The interview distinguishes brevity from shortness: long work can still be brief if every part earns its place. For this skill, the relevant control is not word count but causal necessity.

Agent rule:

- Keep assumptions, lore, and emotional logic only if they help the immediate decision.
- Cut reasoning that merely sounds smart.

### Edit Sentence By Sentence

Housel describes editing at sentence level while drafting, with awareness that "perfect" is subjective. For an autonomous fiction agent, this becomes a local verification habit.

Agent rule:

- After drafting or revising, check the exact beat just touched.
- Do not perform global rewrites when the problem is local.

### Respect The Reader Experience

The transcript frames writing as reader empathy: knowing what the writer likes as a reader and reducing friction. For story work, that means protecting orientation and continuity.

Agent rule:

- Track what the reader currently believes.
- Do not surprise the reader by breaking hidden continuity; surprise by recontextualizing known facts.
