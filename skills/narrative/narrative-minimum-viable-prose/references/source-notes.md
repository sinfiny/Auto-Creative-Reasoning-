## Transcript-Derived Principles

### Make Feeling Concrete

The Alain de Botton interview frames writing as turning sensations and emotions into words so they become graspable. For fiction, do not state "she felt devastated" and move on; find the concrete pressure, object, bodily cue, or decision that makes the feeling legible.

Agent rule:

- Convert abstract emotion into noticed detail, action, silence, or choice.

### Some Feelings Are Not Ready

The transcript stresses that a feeling must be understood enough to introduce it to a stranger. In fiction terms, a scene is not ready when the agent cannot explain the emotional beat without contradiction.

Agent rule:

- If the emotional turn is muddy, plan or ask before drafting.

### Attention Finds Hidden Detail

The interview emphasizes looking longer and noticing what was missed at first. This does not mean adding more detail; it means selecting the right detail.

Agent rule:

- Add fewer details, but make each one reveal pressure, value, character, or consequence.

### The Inner Reader Matters

Alain de Botton describes an internal reader who asks how material might fit into another person's life. Morgan Housel similarly emphasizes reader empathy and low-friction reading.

Agent rule:

- Keep the scene readable from the reader's current knowledge.
- Do not make the reader carry lore that does not pay rent in the scene.

### AI Must Not Become Generic

The Alain de Botton transcript explicitly warns that AI can reproduce what has already been said but cannot know the author's private feeling. The craft response is to honor distinctive feeling rather than outsource the core intent.

Agent rule:

- Preserve the user's odd, personal, and specific story signal.
- Avoid replacing it with smooth generic prose.

### Brevity Is No Rambling

Morgan Housel's brevity principle is not "always short"; it is "no wasted motion." For fiction, a long scene can be viable if every beat turns pressure, character, or knowledge.

Agent rule:

- Cut passages readers would skip: repeated emotion, unused lore, ornamental weather, redundant dialogue, and worldbuilding without consequence.
