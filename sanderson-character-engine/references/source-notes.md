# Source Notes

Primary lectures:

- Lecture 6: proactive, relatable, and capable characters
- Lecture 7: customizing your character
- Lecture 8: types of character arcs

Condensed notes:

- Reader attachment can come from multiple levers and the right mix depends on story type.
- Proactivity is often decisive because it creates visible story motion.
- Capability can be intellectual, social, emotional, moral, or physical.
- Relatability grows from understandable motive, pressure, and vulnerability.
- Fascination can replace or supplement likability.
- Character arcs can be positive, flat, tragic, corruptive, or revelatory.
- A concrete inability or refusal early often sets up the later proof of change.
- Character design affects scope, pacing, and how much world the story must carry.

Operational implications:

- When a character feels weak, inspect lever balance before rewriting the whole cast.
- When the user asks for an arc, offer the best-fitting arc type rather than defaulting to personal growth.
- When multiple POVs blur together, separate them by motive, method, and narrative job.
